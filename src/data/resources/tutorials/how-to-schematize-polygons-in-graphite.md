## Introduction

Schematized polygons are essential elements in cartography, providing simplified and stylized representations of geographic areas. Custom schematized polygons can enhance the aesthetic appeal of your maps and improve user experience. In this tutorial, we will explore the steps to create your own schematized polygons using the open source vector software Graphite.

## Step 1: The Software

Graphite is a free vector graphics editor that offers a user-friendly interface and a variety of tools for creating schematized polygons. It's a great option for beginners who want to get started with polygon design without investing in expensive software.

A big plus of Graphite is the node-based editing system, which allows you to manipulate the underlying structure of your polygons with precision. This can be particularly useful for creating complex shapes and ensuring that your schematized polygons are scalable without losing quality. However, it may have a steeper learning curve compared to other software, so be prepared to spend some time getting familiar with its features. If you're familiar with Blender, you'll find the node editor in Graphite to be a powerful tool for polygon design, allowing you to create intricate designs with ease.

## Step 2: Choose the projection

Before you start designing, decide on the projection you want to use for your map. The choice of projection can affect the appearance of your schematized polygons, so it's important to choose one that suits your needs. Common projections include Mercator, Robinson, and Albers Equal-Area. Each projection has its own advantages and disadvantages, so take some time to research and choose the one that best fits your project. Bear in mind that some projections may distort the area or shape of your polygons, so consider the trade-offs when making your decision.

## Step 3: Define Your Polygon Set

Before you start designing, decide on the types of polygons you want to create. Common polygon types include:

- Administrative boundaries (e.g., countries, states, cities)
- Land use (e.g., residential, commercial, industrial)
- Natural features (e.g., forests, lakes, mountains)

The choice of polygon types will depend on the purpose of your map and the information you want to convey. Consider the level of detail you want to include and the overall aesthetic you want to achieve when defining your polygon set.

## Step 4: Draw your polygons

Draw them, seriously. Unfortunately, there's no secret to it, just use the pen tool and draw them. You can use the node editor to adjust the shape and position of your polygons as needed. Remember to keep your design simple and clean, as schematized polygons are meant to be stylized representations rather than detailed depictions.
